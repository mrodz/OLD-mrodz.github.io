The Tropical Grasslands

Ecology Group Project
11/8/2021 — Mateo Rodriguez, Hayden Frintrup, Yanga Booker
Home

Welcome to the Wild! Here, our team hopes to inform you on the wonderful biome that is the Tropical grasslands.

Tropical grasslands are often sandwiched right in the middle of tropical rainforests, which predictably receive large amounts of precipitation every year, and desert biomes, which on the contrary, receive little to no rain at all. They tend to be placed in warm regions that have a rainy and humid wet season, alongside a dry season susceptible to droughts and fires.

The prime real-world location in which Tropical grasslands exist are the Savannas of the Sub-Saharan Desert, and many experts believe that this biome makes up the majority of continental Africa.

Above is a picture of a Tropical Grasslands ecosystem.

In terms of land composition, they have a very wide, open, and flat terrain that allows for a lot of movement from their animal populations. Its climate follows the trend of this biome’s minimal difference in temperature throughout the mapping of the seasons; summers range from 78° to 86° fahrenheit, and winters are between 68° and 78° fahrenheit.

There are many attractions to be enjoyed in the Tropical grasslands; one can partake in a safari tour, camp under a sky blanketed with stars, and learn about the native people and their intricate cultures.

Biome Information

Tropical grasslands are also known as savannas, and they comprise of a mixture of trees and grasses; the proportion between these varies directly with the rainfall. Most of the mammals of the savanna are ground-living species, but there are many a primate to be found in the trees. Tropical grasslands are found exclusively in only three regions of the Earth: Africa, Australia, and some parts of Asia.

Included is a climatogram, which is a type of graph that maps rainfall to average temperature. This one in particular is of the Serengeti region in Tanzania: one of the largest tropical grasslands in the entire world.
Biodiversity Information

In the savannah, one can spot a multitude of recognizable animals that include lions, cheetahs, hyenas, zebras, gazelles, elephants, giraffes, wildebeests and warthogs.

Aside from these renowned creatures, the area contains a fragmented amount of vegetation; wild grass dominates the overall composition of the producers in this scope, but many trees and small shrubs are also found dotted across the land.

The reason for this scarcity can be traced to the lack of rain, for the most part. Despite this, however, some species have managed to thrive.

A list of the most prominent producers are:

  • Elephant grass
  • Senegal gum acacia
  • Umbrella thorn acacia

Back to the topic of the animals of the grasslands, these include:

  • Elephants
  • Giraffes
  • Zebras
  • Rhinoceroses
  • Buffalo
  • Lions
  • Leopards
  • Cheetahs

The overcrowding of herbivores and poaching run a terrible cost to the delicate balance in this ecosystem, though. According to ScienceAdvances, over 30% of the Tropical grassland’s flora is potentially threatened with extinction. Also, The Guardian writes that around 144,000 animals were lost over a seven-year period in 15 African countries, declining at a rate of 8% a year.

One must not lose hope, though, for things are starting to turn themselves around.
  • The country of Botswana is at the top of the list of countries that are doing the most to protect wildlife. It has the highest amount of land reserved for conservation in Africa, and more than a quarter of its land area is allocated specifically for parks and other reserves.
  • Namibia ranks second in the list. The country is so serious about wildlife conservation that certain laws are even included in its constitution.
  • Another honorable mention is Tanzania, who is dedicated to protecting their animals in the wild enough that over a third of this vast nation is protected by legislation.
Endangered Species

Unfortunately, even the average person can observe the teetering of the delicate balance between wildlife and the factors that constantly risk tipping the whole system over on its side.

Many animals have been affected by this ongoing struggle, and some have even come to be labeled as endangered.

  • Grévy's Zebra
Grévy's Zebra

    In 2011, this breed of zebra came dangerously close to extinction; with less than 2,000 of this species left in the wild, it made the Grévy’s zebra the most threatened of any type of zebra.

    Where more than 15,000 once lived in countries such as Kenya and Ethiopia, this zebra’s population has been steadily declining thanks to the destruction of its natural habitat and hunting by humans. Disease and competition with other animals has also added to this poor creature's woes.

    In actuality, the Grévy's zebras tend to mingle on the open plains of the tropical grassland with other grazing animals such as wildebeest, ostriches and antelopes.

    The male variety of Grévy's zebras are solitary in their territories, except for the rare passing of a female during breeding season. Bachelor males, or non-territorial males, have been observed to travel together in bundles of 2 to 6 members. This social system is different from other zebras, which form female harems (female animals sharing a single mate) in a single male's territory all year.

    This class of zebra, naturally, has its species’ trademark black and white stripes, along with long necks and prominent, erect manes. They have the largest ears of any zebra species.

    Grévy's are the largest of all zebras, as well; measuring over 8.9 ft. in length and weighing in well over 900 pounds, this equestrian mammal sure is a force to be reckoned with.

  • Black Rhinoceroses
The Black Rhino

    This breed of rhino could once be found in many parts of Africa, such as Somalia and Namibia, but in 2011, largely due to poaching by humans, it became confined to a measly four countries. Programs have been designed to stabilize populations of black rhinos in the country of South Africa, and because of this, over 40 percent of the animals are now found in said country.

    One of the subspecies of the black rhino, the south-western variety, has disappeared entirely, and was last spotted in Africa in 1853.

    Aside from females and their offspring, black rhinos are solitary. Females reproduce slowly, and breaks between pregnancies can last up to five years. A single calf cannot live on its own until it is about three years old.

    The black rhino is smaller than its counterpart, the white rhino. Adult males can weigh up to 1,350 kilograms, and females up to 900. They also have less of a pronounced hump on the back of their necks. The black rhino species is considered to be browsers, meaning that they eat from higher bushes and trees.

    Black rhinos can be either solitary or territorial, and it all comes down on their habitat. Adult male black rhinos tend to live on their own, except when attempting to court females. Females tend to remain solitary, and are usually only found in the presence of their latest calf.

There are many groups and non-proffit organizations that seek to better the condition of these species by the means of providing conservation funds directly to governments and dispatching teams of volunteers to face these topical issues on the front lines. Human Impact

Savannas are subject to natural wildfires during their dry seasons, but humans are often to blame for some number of fires as well. Farmers also use the land of tropical grasslands to plant their crops, which can be disruptive to the wildlife.

Farms that encroach upon large amounts of animals’ grazing territory can be detrimental to their livelihood, since developing farmland typically starts with clearing out trees. This destroys the habitats of many species that rely on these organisms to survive. Some farmers also allow their livestock to roam freely about the savannas, which only robs food and nutrients from the land’s natural inhabitants.

Poaching is also a major threat to wildlife in the tropical grassland. Land mammals such as elephants, rhinos, and hippos are hunted for their ivory horns, and many more creatures suffer from similar fates all because of a longing for their rare and elusive parts.

In regards to climate change, and more specifically global warming, most tropical grassland regions are suffering from droughts that can be directly linked to the heating of our planet.

These factors are not exclusively pernicious to natural wildlife; they can affect humans as well. Droughts force even the common folk to scrounge harder for potable water, and wildfires caused by blistering temperatures can destroy entire villages. Not to mention that the loss of Africa’s most recognizeable creatures would ruin the economy of various countries that depend on tourism; according to the Internacional Finance Corporation, “tourism has become vital to African economies. In 2019, the industry accounted for about seven percent of Africa's GDP and contributed $169 billion to its economy.” Losing this vital stream of income would surely halt the growth of many developing nations.

Many individuals on the board of the African Wildlife Foundation agree that to preserve many of their continent’s bustling ecosystems, including the tropical grassland, countries should train more experienced rangers and scouts, implement a web of cameras and other sensors to monitor and protect the populations of endangered species, and train their governments to effectively manage protected areas. They also acknowledge that overcoming climate change is an issue that will need to be addressed in the future, though it is not clear how this will be accomplished.

Regardless, analysts predict that these numbers will decrease in the future; increased awareness and global conservation efforts are beginning to show signs of success, and many individuals will continue to do their part in attempting to resolve this issue. As for eliminating the artificial warming of the Earth, scientists are still actively looking for ways to combat climate change, and if they were to be successful, then the effects of this discovery would have a widespread impact that would surely not limit itself to just the savanna.
